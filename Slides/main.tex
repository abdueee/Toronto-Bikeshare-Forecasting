\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Project Overview}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Goal:} Forecast hourly bikeshare pickup demand across Toronto using ML models (Tabular and NNs).

  \item \textbf{Data:} 12 months of ridership (Jan 2025 -- Feb 2026), station
  metadata (GBFS), and hourly weather (Open-Meteo). Split chronologically:
  train on 2025, validate on Jan 2026, test on Feb 2026.

  \item \textbf{Key design choice:} K-means clustering ($K=8$) on station
  coordinates reduces station-level zero inflation from \textbf{77.7\%} to
  \textbf{37.3\%} at the cluster level, enabling meaningful demand signal.

  \item \textbf{Model progression:} Linear Regression (baseline)
  $\rightarrow$ Random Forest and XGBoost $\rightarrow$ PyTorch MLP, evaluated
  on MAE, RMSE, and $R^2$.

  \item \textbf{Best result:} XGBoost (tuned) achieves $R^2 = 0.87$,
  MAE $= 7.15$ on the held-out test set.
\end{itemize}

\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Data Pipeline, EDA, and Feature Engineering}

{\small
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ingestion \& Cleaning:} 12 monthly CSVs (Jan 2025 -- Feb 2026); trips filtered to 1 minute-- 24 hours; aggregated to station-hour pickups with implicit zeros filled.
  \item \textbf{Zero Inflation:} 77.7\% of station-hours have zero pickups -- station-level granularity is too sparse for reliable learning.
  \item \textbf{Clustering:} K-means ($K=8$) on station lat/lon groups 999 stations into 8 geographic clusters, cutting zero inflation to \textbf{37.3\%} and amplifying demand signal.
  \item \textbf{Features \& Split:} 12 features (temporal, weather, \texttt{lag\_24h}); chronological split -- 2025 $\rightarrow$ train, Jan 2026 $\rightarrow$ val, Feb 2026 $\rightarrow$ test.
\end{itemize}
}

}

\vspace{0.3cm}

{\small
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{lag\_24h drives most of the signal} -- without it, test $R^2 = -2.28$; LR has no way to anchor predictions to current demand levels without a direct reference to yesterday.
  \item \textbf{Non-linearity is the ceiling} -- \texttt{month} enters as a single linear coefficient, so LR cannot distinguish summer rush hours from winter rush hours. This is a gap Tabular models and NNs can close.
\end{itemize}
}
